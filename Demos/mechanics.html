Technology Overview & Requirements

This program uses Canvas plus optional external generative AI tools to support learning, experimentation, and reflection. Specific tools may change over time. The learning goal is transferable skill—not mastery of a single platform.

Required technology

  • Access to Canvas
  • A modern web browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge)
  • Reliable internet access
  • Ability to view and create text-based documents

Recommended login strategy: use a Google account when possible

Many tools support “Sign in with Google.” To reduce friction and simplify access, we recommend using a Google account as your primary login method when possible.

  • Fewer passwords and fewer account recovery problems
  • Smoother access across multiple tools
  • Easier document workflows (uploading, saving, and organizing outputs)
  • More consistent experience across devices and sessions

Alternative login options or tools may be used where available. A Google account is recommended, not required.

Accessibility considerations

AI tools vary in accessibility and interface design. If a tool presents accessibility barriers, participants may use an alternative tool or approach to meet the learning objective. The goal is to demonstrate understanding and process—not use a specific interface.

Tip: Start with low-stakes content when trying a new tool. Save sensitive or important work for tools explicitly designated as secure or campus-provided.

Privacy & Data Use

CITL is committed to protecting participant privacy and minimizing data collection while supporting meaningful learning experiences. This section explains how participant data related to this course is collected, used, and protected.

What CITL collects

  • Enrollment and completion status
  • Required submissions or reflections explicitly requested as part of the course
  • Optional feedback or evaluation responses
  • Aggregate participation metrics (for course improvement and reporting)

How CITL uses course-related data

  • Verify certificate completion
  • Improve course design and instructional support
  • Report aggregated participation outcomes to campus stakeholders

What CITL does not collect

  • Activity inside third-party AI tools
  • Prompts, chat histories, uploaded documents, or generated outputs unless you explicitly submit them
  • Access to your personal accounts or files
  • Course data for evaluation of job performance or teaching effectiveness

Use of external AI tools

External AI tools are governed by their own privacy policies and terms of service. CITL does not control how these tools store or process data. Participants are responsible for deciding what to upload and for avoiding sensitive, confidential, or protected information.

Rule of thumb: If you wouldn’t paste it into a public webpage, don’t upload it to a tool unless that tool is explicitly designated as secure or campus-provided for that type of data.

Questions or concerns

Questions about privacy, data use, or participation should be directed through the Canvas course site or the program contact listed on the certificate page.

Communication Expectations

This program emphasizes learning through exploration, iteration, and reflection rather than correctness or compliance. The goal is to build practical skill and sound judgment—especially around when to use AI and when to rely on human expertise.

What you can expect from CITL

  • Clear instructions for activities and deliverables
  • Transparent communication about expectations and changes
  • Respect for participant autonomy and professional judgment
  • Feedback focused on process, clarity, and reflection

Important: CITL does not monitor AI tool usage or record your activity inside third-party tools.

What is expected of participants

  • Engage thoughtfully with course materials and activities
  • Use AI tools as supports—not as authoritative sources
  • Evaluate AI outputs for accuracy, limitations, and fit for purpose
  • Be prepared to explain how AI tools were used in submitted work

How to describe AI use (simple transparency)

When you submit an artifact or reflection, a short note is usually sufficient, such as:

  • “I used AI to generate three draft outlines; I selected one and revised it.”
  • “I used AI to identify potential gaps; I verified claims with external sources.”

Where to Go for Help

Instructional and course support (CITL)

CITL can help with questions about course content and expectations, learning objectives, conceptual use of AI tools, and how to interpret feedback. For help, use the contact pathway provided in the Canvas course site or the program contact listed on the certificate page.

Technical support boundaries

Participants are responsible for creating and managing external tool accounts and for basic operation of third-party tools. CITL does not provide platform-level troubleshooting for external AI tools, but may share general guidance or documentation links when appropriate.

Fastest fix: If a tool isn’t working, try a different browser, sign out/in, or switch tools to complete the learning objective. The course is designed so tool choice is flexible.

What to include when you ask for help

  • What you were trying to do
  • Which tool you used (and whether it was required or optional)
  • What you tried already
  • A screenshot of any error message (if relevant)

Responsible Use & Academic Integrity

Generative AI tools are designed to produce plausible outputs, not verified facts. You remain responsible for accuracy, interpretation, and ethical use. AI can support your work, but it does not replace disciplinary knowledge, contextual judgment, or accountability.

Core principles

  • Verify: Check claims, citations, and factual statements using appropriate sources.
  • Revise: Treat AI output as draft material—shape it to your purpose, audience, and context.
  • Disclose: Be transparent about meaningful AI assistance when submitting course work.
  • Own: You own the decisions and the outcomes—AI does not.

Appropriate use examples

  • Brainstorming options, outlines, and alternative framings
  • Reorganizing or clarifying your own notes or draft ideas
  • Generating drafts that you evaluate and substantially revise
  • Surfacing possible gaps or questions to investigate next

What to avoid

  • Submitting AI output as authoritative without verification
  • Using AI to bypass learning objectives (e.g., outsourcing required thinking)
  • Uploading sensitive or protected data to tools not designated for it

Practical transparency: A short note describing how you used AI is usually sufficient (e.g., “used AI to draft, then revised and verified key claims”).

Still unsure? When in doubt, choose the more privacy-protective option and ask for help through the course site.
